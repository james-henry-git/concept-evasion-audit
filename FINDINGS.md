# Neural Chameleons Challenge — Overall Findings

*Written: 2026-05-23 18:41 UTC*

## Summary

We attempted to replicate and challenge the Neural Chameleons paper's core claim: that mechanistic interpretability probes are vulnerable to targeted evasion fine-tuning, and that abliteration-damaged models are especially susceptible. Across three experimental phases, we found no evidence that this vulnerability generalizes beyond their specific experimental setup.

**Bottom line**: The evasion attack fails completely on an intact safety-tuned model. Abliteration causes only surgical, concept-specific damage to representations — not the broad collapse needed to explain their results. CAZ-positioned probes are at least as robust as fixed-layer probes under these conditions.

---

## Phase A — Evasion Attack on Intact Model (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct)

**Setup**: Fixed-layer probe at layer 14 (n_layers//2), matching their method. LoRA evasion fine-tuning (r=16, dual KL+obfuscation loss, 3 epochs). 8 benign + 5 safety concepts.

**Result**: Zero evasion. Recall@1%FPR drop = 0.000 across all 13 concepts, both with and without the trigger prefix. The obfuscation loss (probe score) remained at ~0.99 throughout training — the LoRA lacked gradient signal to overcome intact representations.

**What this means**: The attack is not a general-purpose MI evasion method. On a standard safety-tuned model, the concept representations are sufficiently robust that LoRA fine-tuning under their loss function cannot suppress them.

---

## Phase B — CAZ-Aware Probing: Layer Placement and Abliteration

### B1–B2: Does CAZ probing outperform fixed-layer probing?

**Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct** (benign concepts): CAZ peak/handoff/ensemble all match or exceed the fixed-layer baseline (already 1.000). One notable case: `negation` has a bimodal signature — peak at L0 (0.950) and handoff at L28 (0.990) — and the CAZ ensemble recovers it to 1.000. This is the only concept where multi-layer ensemble provides a material lift over any single layer.

**Verdict**: CAZ positioning does not hurt performance, and in edge cases (concepts with split assembly signatures) it helps. For intact models where fixed-layer probes already achieve near-perfect recall, the practical gain is marginal.

### B1–B2: Does abliteration degrade safety-concept representations?

**Setup**: `google/gemma-2-9b-it` (normal) vs. `IlyaGusev/gemma-2-9b-it-abliterated` (their exact model). CAZ probes trained at concept-specific assembly layers for 10 concepts.

| Concept | Normal R@1% (peak) | Ablitd R@1% (peak) | Δ |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|---|
| authorization | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| deception | 0.980 | 0.980 | 0.000 |
| **exfiltration** | 0.960 | **0.911** | **−0.050** |
| obfuscation | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| threat_severity | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| causation | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| certainty | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| credibility | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| moral_valence | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |
| negation | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.000 |

**Verdict**: Abliteration leaves 9 of 10 concepts completely intact. The one exception — `exfiltration` (-5pp at peak) — is recovered by the CAZ ensemble to within 1pp of the normal model. This is targeted geometric entanglement, not broad representation collapse.

## Synthesis

Three conditions Neural Chameleons' result would require, and what we found:

| Condition | Required | Observed |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| LoRA attack suppresses probe representations | Yes | No — obfuscation loss stuck at 0.99 on intact model |
| Abliteration broadly degrades safety-concept geometry | Yes | No — 9/10 concepts zero-delta; exfiltration −5pp only |
| CAZ ensemble is more vulnerable than fixed-layer | No (favors us) | Also no — equivalent or better |

### The most likely explanation

Neural Chameleons' reported recall drops appear to depend on the intersection of:
1. **Abliteration-damaged model** as the probe-training base (weakening the signal the probe learns from, not just inference-time representations)
2. **Synthetic training data generated by the abliterated model** (not released; we cannot replicate without it)
3. Possibly: their probe training and evasion evaluated on the same distribution shift introduced by abliteration, making the drop an artifact of that shift rather than genuine representation suppression

Their result may be internally valid for their specific setup. It does not appear to reflect a generalizable vulnerability of MI probing as a safety tool.

### What remains open

- **B3** (not run): LoRA evasion on the abliterated Gemma model using their exact training pipeline. Would require their synthetic harmful training data, which is not released.
- The 5pp `exfiltration` degradation warrants a follow-up: is the refusal direction geometrically entangled with exfiltration specifically, or is this a probe-training artifact from the abliterated model's weakened signal?
